2. Auditory Profile: Rainfall on Wooden House Surfaces

Raindrops contacting wooden house surfaces — whether roof planking, exterior wall boards, window frames, or structural overhangs — produce a warm, organic tap with natural timber resonance that varies subtly according to wood species, plank thickness, weathering degree, and surface orientation. Aged exterior wood, having undergone years of seasonal moisture cycling and surface weathering, develops a softened surface layer that slightly attenuates raindrop impact sharpness while allowing the underlying timber mass to resonate with full warmth and body. Frequency output centers between 350–2,000 Hz across wooden surface types, producing a consistently warm low-to-mid acoustic profile that the human auditory system processes as deeply organic, materially grounded, and inherently comforting.
2.2 Multi-Surface Acoustic Complexity
The wooden house as a rain-receiving structure produces a uniquely complex acoustic environment through simultaneous rainfall contact across multiple surface types and orientations. Roof surfaces receive rainfall at perpendicular impact, generating the fullest resonance tones. Vertical wall planking receives rainfall at oblique angles, producing softer, more diffuse tap patterns. Window frames and structural trim elements contribute shorter, more defined tap sounds at higher frequencies. This multi-surface acoustic layering creates a three-dimensional rainfall soundscape of extraordinary richness — each surface contributing its distinctive tonal character to an integrated acoustic whole that surrounds the interior occupant from above and all sides simultaneously.
